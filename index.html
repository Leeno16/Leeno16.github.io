For this interaction design project, I worked as part of a team that used a design (or cultural) probe to gather information around the issue of food waste, with a focus on third level students and their habits. This design technique was part of a strategy of pursuing experimental design in a responsive way and allow the gathering of sufficient data and information.  Simply put, a design probe is a combination of artifacts and materials with certain tasks to be preformed by the participants/users. They enable a designer/researcher to gather subjective information on participants (who self document) and can be very useful, especially early in the design phase.

For this project we created a simple probe kit (as seen in the slideshow) consisting of a diary, log in details to an instagram account (to upload pictures from a users smartphone), fridge magnets as a reminder to complete tasks, a questionnaire and also information/task cards. These were held together in a lunch box type container to keep with the food waste theme. The results of this probe gave us invaluable insights that we otherwise would not have had. Unlike direct observation, it allowed the users to self report without worrying about things like being observed. The analysis and subsequent results highlighted several problem areas. As this is an early part in the design phase, it allows the overall project to go in a few different directions. From example personas, empathy maps or proptypes can be created from the information gathered. As a group we recommended creating and testing a prototype of a mobile phone app that would aid and help third level students combat food waste.

  • Project Type: Group work
  • Design Techniques & Practices: Interaction Design, Design Research, Task Analysis, User Analysis

The interactive multimedia module in the third year of my course, gave me the opportunity to develop an interactive application in Processing, while also designing elements of the application with Photoshop and Illustrator. The application has a window where the user can click. Each click represents a simple synthesised sound, with the vertical location controlling pitch and horizontal controlling timing. When clicks are activated and replayed, in a loop, a visual representation of each sound is animated. The project followed the following process.

The application initially was just a simple screen, which the user could click and blobs would appear, making a sound as they faded away and then reappearing, in a loop, in the order the user had clicked the screen. This was improved upon and the first updated version of the application, featured an interface with control buttons, added functionality, and also had a small play area where the user could click to activate the blobs and their sounds.

To test and evaluate the application, I carried out co-operative evaluations with several users. These evaluations had users perform different tasks with the application. Users were also asked to think aloud, in order to understand their thought process and find out exactly what their expectations were, while also identifying what aspects of the system were confusing. I also used Nielsen's Ten Heuristics for user interface design, to improve the application's design further. These were important aspects of the project, as it allowed for good feedback from different users and in turn improved the overall design and functionality of the application.

A product design module in second year of my course focused on research and design specification. Working as part of a group I had to carry out research around the area of travel in UL with the aim to encourage smarter travel (cycling, walking, car pooling) in the college.

To do this the project focused on the various transition areas on UL campus, the relevant stakeholders/users and the issues they encountered regularly. Ideas for these transition areas were to be developed while all the time keeping the needs of walkers and cyclists in mind. The basis of this project was the Limerick Smarter Travel (LST) initiative. This LST project was researched initially to gain an understanding of what would be required for this overall project. To have empathy with the various stakeholders/users and to understand their needs, various different research stages were carried out. Human centred and ethnographic research methods were used, documenting the process of discovery and field work carried out. This eventually led towards a design guide and potential prototypes to help the various stakeholders.

My Group's Strategic Focus Statement was as follows:
“To create a better experience for new and current cyclists and walkers by breaking down the barriers that obstructs these forms of travel.”

The project's process followed several steps along the way and included methods such as observations, information walls, brainstorming and persona creation. It's goal was to find solutions for the problems observed and encountered. It offered a good opportunity to use these different methods of team work and research in the design industry. As a group we documented our findings and solutions in a design guide, which also outlined the various needs of the relevant people involved and the different issues encountered.

This group project undertook a Cooperative Evaluation of the website Taptu.com. Taptu was a RSS news feed, which is at the time was relatively new and was an alternative to similar applications such as Google Reader, Feedly and Flipboard. Taptu was a cross platform feed aggregator. It allowed the user to link in and share news feeds on their social media. For this project, a meticulous process was followed, which began with the planning of the evaluation exercise, a set of test tasks and moved on to the gathering of results, an analysis from the evaluation and re-design recommendations.

The planning process included looking at which scenarios and tasks would be put to the user to be carried out, who these participants and users would be, the different ethical issues, how information would be recorded during the evaluation and how the results would be analysed. All this was aided by looking at examples from previous research and literature into the cooperative evaluation process. From the information collected and the evaluated results, re-design considerations were put forth. This was done by choosing the five major usability problems and proposing a redesign of these, based on the design and usability principles covered during the HCI module.

The method of cooperative evaluation is similar to that of the think aloud method. Here both the evaluator and the user can communicate and ask each other questions while carrying out the evaluation. The evaluator sets the user certain tasks to be carried out to evaluate a system. The method draws on the think aloud technique by having the user describe these tasks that they are carrying out, why they are doing them and what they think is occurring at a particular point in the process. The user may want clarification or have a problem with something specific, that the evaluator can help with. Also the evaluator can ask questions to help better evaluate the situation. Therefore the evaluator can participate and help when needed (clarify points of confusion, etc.), while the user can freely criticise the system instead of being afraid to be negative and thus the evaluation will be more accurate. The results from this method can bring about a large amount of information which has to be analysed and this can take up a lot of time and be very tedious at times. However the relaxed and open nature of the method allows for an accurate evaluation with good contributions from the user and evaluator.

​
  • Project Type: Group work
  • Design Techniques & Practices: Usability Testing, Co-operative Evaluations, Task Analysis

Interaction Design - Rapid Prototyping

This interaction design project followed on from the design probe phase. As a group we used the insights and information gathered to design a prototype to help students deal with food waste. A task analysis was conducted and a prototype of a mobile phone app was decided to be the most appropriate. The overall project involved many steps and the rapid and paper prototyping process can be seen below.

There are different techniques for rapid prototyping and storyboarding is one such technique. For this project storyboards were used to see how the intended apps interactions and features would play out in a real environment. The visual nature of storyboards allows for the communication of these user interactions. Each of the following storyboards and scenarios demonstrates the proposed application’s differing role in aiding students deal with food waste.

The goal of creating sketches was to use them to create a low fidelity paper prototype. This allows for quick iterations and changes to the early designs. Paper Prototyping is a technique that consists of creating hand drawings of user interfaces in order to enable them to be rapidly designed, simulated and tested. Another advantage of using paper, when testing, the user might not feel obliged to hold back when criticising the prototyping when looking at paper.

FYP - Adequacy of Computer Backup Strategies

For my FYP I carried out a research project, exploring the adequacy of peoples backup strategies. This involved a literature review and state of the art as well as carrying out empirical research in the form of a survey to assess peoples strategies and backup practices. People of different ages and computer literacy were targeted for this survey.

Results showed interesting backup practices and issues across various levels and ages. General takeaways from the study confirmed the need for people to know exactly what data is important to them and where it exists, as data loss can occur for a variety of reasons and at anytime. Time and convenience are barriers preventing sound strategies from being in place while a lack of knowledge impedes those who are less computer literate.

A quick breakdown of the survey results, showed that older people are less likely to have an adequate backup strategy, however those who are computer literate are more likely to have one. 52% of all participants ,rated their current strategies as ‘Poor’ or worse while 46% of those who tested their strategy, rated it as ‘Good’ or better. Testing ones backup strategy therefore would seem to be important and a key component of any good backup strategy.

IBM Workshop - Empathy Maps

I attended a design workshop held by IBM, in my the fourth year of Digital Media Design. The workshop proved to be very insightful and gave the class the opportunity to create an “Empathy Map” for a hypothetical user travelling to Mars. The map was then presented at the end of the task. An empathy map is simply a map of all the possible thoughts and emotions a person may feel about an event or an external factor.

Empathy maps are useful when there is a need to think systematically about users and all of their attributes, beyond just their job title or role. They can be used to capture the current state of knowledge of a product/service and uncover assumptions that can be tested and validated through research. Empathy maps are a user centric method that provides a good structure for talking about users.

During the workshop, each group was assigned a specified character and my group was given a man who worked as a barista. Groups could then flesh out the biography of that character and the barista became a 32 year old man, who had a wife and one child. To complete the maps, groups added what their character was saying, doing, seeing and thinking and compiled the information into somewhat of a narrative about their upcoming trip to Mars. Since groups had different characters, the empathy maps were interesting and varied and showed how useful they can be as part of the collaborative design tool to gain deeper insights into users.
